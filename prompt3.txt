Research framework:
- Treat alpha, execution, and risk as separate models.
- Alpha model predicts edge direction and magnitude.
- Execution model predicts fill probability, slippage, impact, and fail-to-fill behavior.
- Risk model enforces inventory bounds, drawdown brakes, venue limits, liquidation distance, and kill switches.
- Do not accept any result that depends on optimistic mid-price fills.

Execution realism requirements:
- maker/taker fees and rebates
- spread cost
- slippage
- impact
- adverse selection
- latency and jitter
- queue effects where available
- partial fills
- cancel/replace latency
- fail-to-fill behavior
- funding / basis drift where relevant

Research questions to answer:
- Which strategy families show real edge after full costs?
- Which horizons work: seconds, minutes, hours, multi-hour, 8h-72h?
- Which parameter regions are stable rather than locally overfit?
- Which indicators are causative, which are only correlative?
- Where does edge survive across:
  - low/high volatility
  - liquidity buckets
  - event/jump windows
  - different symbols / sectors
- Where does backtest diverge from forward or paper behavior?

Promotion ladder:
idea -> offline backtest -> walk-forward -> paper / shadow -> testnet infra validation -> small-risk live

Promotion criteria:
- positive realized edge after full costs
- positive realized return
- sufficient sample size in backtest and forward windows
- acceptable fill ratio
- acceptable drawdown
- stable behavior across regime slices
- tolerable live-vs-backtest drift
- no dependency on unrealistic execution assumptions

Kill criteria:
- edge disappears after realistic costs
- edge exists only in a narrow regime with no plausible live detector
- forward behavior contradicts backtest
- results are driven by sparse trades
- profitability depends on optimistic fill assumptions
- strategy complexity exceeds expected practical payoff
